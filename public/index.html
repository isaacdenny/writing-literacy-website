Do it yourself.ai

A story by Isaac Denny

What has been your experience with AI?
Ask ChatGPT for the best way to learn a skill. You might receive suggestions like focused practice and active learning, or perhaps repetition and consistency. What our AI helpers do not know is that they are inhibiting the learning ability of an entire generation of programmers. Unfortunately, many LLM users outsource their essay-writing, email-writing, programming, or even texting to the virtual assistant without a second thought. Climbing out of the addictive, vicious cycle that is asking ChatGPT for help with writing code was my biggest challenge when learning to code in my first year of college.
It's just a tool, what's the big deal?
I had no understanding of what I was writing. The first semester of my freshman year mostly involved gawking at coding challenges on LeetCode, trying my best to design algorithms to solve them, and inevitably asking ChatGPT for help when I hit an obstacle. Many nights I sat in my dark, honors dorm room at Boise State University, Idaho and struggled with the difficult principles of computer science. As far as I could tell, AI frequently answered my low-difficulty questions correctly; countless bugs would sneak under my inexperienced radar. It was a formula for poorly-written, untested code that kept me coming back for more.
Why use it, then?
Taking the first step away from ChatGPT was daunting. As I scrolled through the impressive resume projects of job-crushing college-grads and freelance workaholics alike, I shook at the sheer number of work-hours necessary to complete such tasks without assistance. I frequently found myself questioning my ability without an AI assistant backing my play, but deep down I knew if I wanted to get any good at programming, I needed to take a step away from prompting.
How did you overcome this?
I started with a simple form in the sunlit, fourth-floor common room of my building at BSU. My fiance and I wanted a website to announce our wedding and register guests: this seemed like the perfect opportunity to learn how to build a website from scratch, without ChatGPT. Contrary to popular understanding of computer scientists, I need sunlight to write code. I much preferred the bright, floor-to-ceiling-windowed common room in the honors dorm building at BSU to my small, dimly-lit dorm room and reserved roommate. That is where I built my first website: a simple registration form for my wedding in June of 2023. The feeling of accomplishment was my own; it gave me a step toward AI independence.
Surely it's not that simple
The more code I wrote on my own, the more I understood. Soon after finishing my wedding website, I received a job building a website for a local company, then another job building internal tooling. My learning speed increased, and soon I had a basic understanding of the principles of programming on the web. For me, the project or tool was less important than the ability to build an idea and reach a goal without assistance. When I first learned to write code, the language server or text editor tools hindered how quickly I could pick up a language. Likewise, AI tools like Copilot or Tabnine that constantly try to guess what you are thinking while you type were counterproductive and unhealthy during my first steps away from ChatGPT: it was too easy to get the answer and move on without considering if it was bug-infested (and it often was).
Is this a one-off problem?
I can see now that I was not alone in my addiction. In each of my middle-to-higher-level classes, many of my classmates race to the comforting OpenAI website to ask for solutions to their problems, or help building out a project as I once did. As more AI tools promise to replace programmers, their appeal adds to the difficulty of breaking free: why learn to code when an AI tool can spit out the correct answer and save you time? I shudder every time I remember the many long hours staring at error messages and fixing bugs without a second robotic opinion, but those late-night coding sessions fueled my learning. Even though my projects were often unfinished and lost to the depths of my computerâ€™s hard drive, their invaluable lesson was never lost to me: do it yourself.
I can see your point
By far, I write more code than any other medium (including text messages), and while it is powerfully tempting to ask ChatGPT to write everything for me, the side-effect of losing all ability to write is too costly. I can write for hours at a time to build out an idea or see a project through, but, as you have seen, I did not start that way. Learning to write code has been one of the most influential and empowering experiences of my life and career, and continues to be as I write more. At the simplest level, it has given me a greater respect for the systems our society has created, the systems we use every day.
